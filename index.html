A Brief History

The Quantum Leap

Quantum theory, born in the 1920s, introduced the concept of **wave-particle duality**, which became the basis for **digital computers**. These machines replaced **human computers**, who were previously tasked with complex, tedious calculations. This shift highlights how foundational scientific discoveries can lead to major societal transformations.

From Bits to Breakthroughs

Quantum computing began by replacing bits with qubits, leading to the creation of the first theoretical quantum computer in 1980. The field gained global attention with Shor's algorithm, which demonstrated the power of quantum computing by solving complex encryption problems.

Quantum Supremacy Claim

In 2019, Google AI and NASA claimed to have achieved quantum supremacy using a 54-qubit machine, performing a calculation that no traditional computer could. The claim's validity, however, is still under review.

Harvard's Quantum Leap

In December 2023, scientists from Harvard University successfully created "quantum circuits" that correct errors more efficiently than alternative methods, which may remove a major obstacle to practical quantum computers.

Core Principles

Quantum computing is built on three pillars: *superposition, **entanglement, and **quantum tunneling*. Superposition allows a quantum bit (qubit) to be in multiple states simultaneously. Entanglement links the states of multiple qubits, regardless of distance, leading to massive parallelism.

Key Algorithms

Two of the most famous quantum algorithms are *Shor's algorithm* and *Grover's algorithm*. Shor's algorithm can find the prime factors of a large number exponentially faster than classical computers, which poses a threat to current encryption methods. Grover's algorithm can search an unstructured database much faster than its classical counterparts.

Real-World Applications

The potential of quantum computing is vast. It can revolutionize fields like *drug discovery* by simulating complex molecular interactions, optimize intricate *financial models, and enable breakthroughs in **materials science* by designing new, more efficient materials.
